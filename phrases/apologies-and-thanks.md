# Apologies & Thanks

Japanese threads gratitude and apology much more densely through daily interaction than English does. Learning the register range here will make you sound noticeably more fluent than your vocabulary alone.

## Thanks — from casual to formal

- **ありがとう** (arigatou) — casual "thanks."
- **ありがとうございます** (arigatou gozaimasu) — polite "thank you" (present; for ongoing/about-to-happen help).
- **ありがとうございました** (arigatou gozaimashita) — polite "thank you" for something now completed.
- **どうもありがとうございます** (doumo arigatou gozaimasu) — a more emphatic thank you.
- **本当にありがとうございます** (hontou ni arigatou gozaimasu) = "thank you really/truly."
- **どうも** alone — casual, "thanks" (almost like "cheers"). Friendly but not suited for all contexts.

## Responding to thanks

- **いいえ** (iie) = literally "no" — used like "not at all."
- **どういたしまして** (dou itashimashite) — "you're welcome," but it can sound slightly formal/textbook. Many Japanese speakers soften with:
- **いえいえ** (ie ie) — "no no, don't mention it."
- **とんでもないです** (tondemonai desu) — "not at all, it's nothing."

## Apologies — from casual to formal

- **ごめん** (gomen) — casual "sorry" (close friends/family).
- **ごめんなさい** (gomen nasai) — "I'm sorry" (still fairly personal).
- **すみません** (sumimasen) — apologetic "excuse me / sorry," workhorse of daily life. Also doubles as "thanks for the trouble."
- **失礼しました** (shitsurei shimashita) — "I was rude / pardon me" — said after a small breach (interrupting, leaving early).
- **申し訳ありません** (moushiwake arimasen) — "I have no excuse" — very formal, business apology.
- **申し訳ございません** (moushiwake gozaimasen) — even more formal.

## Sumimasen as hybrid gratitude

When someone does you a favor that inconvenienced them, Japanese speakers often say **すみません** instead of or alongside ありがとう. It acknowledges the effort they spent. Leaning into this is a marker of cultural fluency.

- (Someone holds a door for you with hands full.) すみません、ありがとうございます。

## Responding to apologies

- **大丈夫です** (daijoubu desu) = "it's fine / no problem."
- **気にしないでください** (ki ni shinaide kudasai) = "please don't worry about it."
- **いいえ、こちらこそ** (iie, kochira koso) = "no, the other way around / I should apologize."
