# Greetings & Farewells

The social choreography of opening and closing interactions in Japanese. Notice how time-of-day matters and how the phrase you choose signals how much you're going to see this person again.

## Time-gated greetings

- **おはようございます** (ohayou gozaimasu) — "Good morning" (until ~10am). Casual: おはよう.
- **こんにちは** (konnichiwa) — "Hello / good afternoon." Daytime default.
- **こんばんは** (konbanwa) — "Good evening." Early evening onward.

## Meeting someone for the first time

```
はじめまして。[name] と申します。よろしくお願いします。
```

- **はじめまして** (hajimemashite) = "Nice to meet you" (first meeting only — never used again with the same person).
- **～と申します** (to moushimasu) = "I am called ~" — a humble self-introduction. Casual alternative: **～です**.
- **よろしくお願いします** (yoroshiku onegai shimasu) = "Please treat me well / I look forward to this."

## Asking how someone is

- **お元気ですか？** (ogenki desu ka?) = "Are you well?" — used less casually than English "how are you?"; often reserved for people you haven't seen in a while.
- **元気です** (genki desu) = "I'm well."
- **まあまあです** (maa maa desu) = "So-so."

## Parting

- **さようなら** (sayounara) — "Goodbye." Formal or somewhat final; less common day-to-day.
- **じゃあね** / **じゃ、また** (jaa ne / ja, mata) — "See you / later." Casual.
- **また明日** (mata ashita) — "See you tomorrow."
- **また来週** (mata raishuu) — "See you next week."
- **お疲れ様でした** (otsukaresama deshita) — literally "you must be tired"; functionally "good job / thanks for today." Said when leaving work or finishing a shared task with colleagues.
- **失礼します** (shitsurei shimasu) — "Excuse me (for leaving)" — when leaving a room or hanging up a phone.

## Entering and leaving home

- **ただいま** (tadaima) — "I'm home" (said by the returner).
- **お帰りなさい** (okaerinasai) — "Welcome back" (said by those already home). Casual: お帰り.
- **行ってきます** (ittekimasu) — "I'm heading out (and will come back)."
- **行ってらっしゃい** (itterasshai) — "Go and come back safely."

These four form two matched pairs; knowing them instantly marks you as someone who's paid attention to Japanese daily life.
