# Numbers

Japanese has two number systems: the **Sino-Japanese** set (borrowed from Chinese, written with kanji) and a **native Japanese** set (used for counting small quantities of things). Start with Sino-Japanese 1–10 — that is what you use for prices, phone numbers, time, and most counters.

## ichi

**一** (ichi) — 1.

## ni

**二** (ni) — 2.

## san

**三** (san) — 3.

## yon / shi

**四** (yon / shi) — 4.
Both readings exist; `yon` is more common in counting out loud because `shi` is a homophone of 死 "death." In time, you'll hear 四時 = *yo*ji (4 o'clock).

## go

**五** (go) — 5.

## roku

**六** (roku) — 6.

## nana / shichi

**七** (nana / shichi) — 7.
Like 4, there's a homophone anxiety: shichi sounds like a compound with 死. `nana` is the safer everyday choice.

## hachi

**八** (hachi) — 8.

## kyuu / ku

**九** (kyuu / ku) — 9.
`kyuu` is default in counting; `ku` shows up in fixed readings like 九時 (kuji, 9 o'clock).

## juu

**十** (juu) — 10.

## Counting 11–20 and beyond

Base ten is easy once you have 1–10:
- 11 = 十一 (juu-ichi) — "ten-one"
- 12 = 十二 (juu-ni)
- 20 = 二十 (ni-juu) — "two-ten"
- 21 = 二十一 (ni-juu-ichi)
- 99 = 九十九 (kyuu-juu-kyuu)
- 100 = 百 (hyaku)
- 1000 = 千 (sen)
- 10,000 = 万 (man) — note: Japanese groups in 10,000s, not 1,000s. 100,000 is 十万 (juu-man).

## Counters (brief intro)

Japanese doesn't say "three books" — it says something closer to "book-three-bound-things." Every category of noun has its own counter suffix. You'll learn counters gradually; here are the three highest-value ones:

- **～つ** (-tsu) — generic counter for abstract or small objects (up to 10). Uses the native numbers: ひとつ, ふたつ, みっつ, よっつ, いつつ, むっつ, ななつ, やっつ, ここのつ, とお.
- **～人** (-nin) — people. 一人 (hitori, 1 person), 二人 (futari, 2 people), then regular: 三人 (san-nin), 四人 (yo-nin)…
- **～個** (-ko) — small round/discrete objects. 一個 (ikko), 二個 (ni-ko)…

For phase 1, prioritize ～つ — it will get you through almost any counting situation as a beginner.
