# Greetings & Polite Phrases

The phrases you hear hundreds of times a day in Japan. Memorize these first — they buy you enormous goodwill even when the rest of your Japanese is still rough.

Format: **Kanji/kana** (romaji) — English. Notes.

## ohayou-gozaimasu

**おはようございます** (ohayou gozaimasu) — "Good morning" (polite).
Drop `ございます` with close friends/family: **おはよう** (ohayou).
Used roughly until 10–11am; after that people switch to `こんにちは`.

## konnichiwa

**こんにちは** (konnichiwa) — "Hello" / "Good afternoon".
The safe all-purpose daytime greeting. Written with は (not わ) because the `は` here is historically the topic particle.

## konbanwa

**こんばんは** (konbanwa) — "Good evening".
Same `は` quirk as above. Used from early evening onward.

## oyasumi-nasai

**おやすみなさい** (oyasumi nasai) — "Good night".
Said when going to bed or parting for the night. Casual: **おやすみ**.

## sayounara

**さようなら** (sayounara) — "Goodbye".
Stronger/more final than English "bye." In daily life Japanese speakers more often use じゃあね (jaa ne) "see ya" or また明日 (mata ashita) "see you tomorrow."

## arigatou

**ありがとう** (arigatou) — "Thanks" (casual).
More polite: **ありがとうございます** (arigatou gozaimasu) — "Thank you."
Past-tense thank-you for a completed favor: **ありがとうございました** (arigatou gozaimashita).

## sumimasen

**すみません** (sumimasen) — "Excuse me" / "Sorry" / "Thanks (for the trouble)."
Extremely versatile. Use to flag a waiter, apologize lightly, or express gratitude when someone goes out of their way.

## hai

**はい** (hai) — "Yes" / "I'm listening" / "Here" (responding to a roll call).
Also used as a neutral acknowledgment — it doesn't always mean agreement.

## iie

**いいえ** (iie) — "No."
Often softened to ううん (uun, casual) or いえ、大丈夫です (ie, daijoubu desu — "no, it's fine") because direct refusal can feel abrupt.

## yoroshiku-onegai-shimasu

**よろしくお願いします** (yoroshiku onegai shimasu) — literally "please treat me favorably"; functionally "nice to meet you" / "I look forward to working with you" / "please do this for me."
One of the most culturally loaded phrases in Japanese. Use it after introductions and after asking for a favor.

## hajimemashite

**はじめまして** (hajimemashite) — "Nice to meet you" (first meeting only).
Paired with よろしくお願いします to close an introduction.

## genki

**元気** (genki) — "well" / "energetic" / "in good health."
`お元気ですか？` (ogenki desu ka?) = "How are you?" — though used less casually than the English version. Answer: はい、元気です (yes, I'm well).
