# Kana: Hiragana & Katakana

Japanese uses three scripts in parallel:
- **Hiragana** (ひらがな) — 46 basic characters; phonetic; used for native grammatical parts of speech (particles, verb endings, native words without kanji).
- **Katakana** (カタカナ) — the same 46 sounds in a second, angular script; used for foreign loanwords, names of foreign origin, onomatopoeia, and emphasis.
- **Kanji** (漢字) — Chinese-derived logographs; carry meaning. You'll layer these in slowly.

Target: comfortable recognition of all 46 hiragana by day 5, all 46 katakana by day 10. Writing can lag behind reading — that's fine.

## The gojūon grid

Both kana follow the same ordering: **a, i, u, e, o** across, and consonant rows **k, s, t, n, h, m, y, r, w** down. Memorize the grid once and both scripts slot into it.

## Dakuten & handakuten

Two tiny diacritics voice a consonant:
- **Dakuten** ( ゛): か→が (ka→ga), さ→ざ (sa→za), た→だ (ta→da), は→ば (ha→ba).
- **Handakuten** ( ゜): only on the h-row to make p-sounds: は→ぱ (ha→pa), ひ→ぴ, ふ→ぷ, へ→ぺ, ほ→ぽ.

## Small-kana combinations (yōon)

A small ゃ・ゅ・ょ glues onto an i-column kana to make a single syllable:
- き + ゃ → きゃ (kya), き + ゅ → きゅ (kyu), き + ょ → きょ (kyo).
- し + ゃ → しゃ (sha), じ + ゃ → じゃ (ja).
- ち + ょ → ちょ (cho).

## Long vowels

A vowel held twice as long is a different word. In hiragana, long vowels are written by adding the matching vowel kana (or う after お and え in many words):
- おかあさん (okaasan) = "mother" — long `a`.
- おにいさん (oniisan) = "older brother" — long `i`.
- せんせい (sensei) = "teacher" — long `e` (spelled -ei).
- がっこう (gakkou) = "school" — long `o` (spelled -ou).

In katakana, long vowels use a simple dash: コーヒー (koohii) = "coffee."

## Small っ (sokuon)

A small っ (or ッ in katakana) doubles the following consonant and creates a short pause:
- きって (kitte) = "stamp" — not "kite."
- がっこう (gakkou) = "school."

## Learning strategy

1. Memorize the **vowel row** (a-i-u-e-o) first — the rest of the chart is just consonant + vowel.
2. Learn hiragana row-by-row (k, s, t, n…) across 3–4 days.
3. Read simple words aloud each day (see daily lessons).
4. Start katakana once hiragana feels sticky. Many katakana shapes are mirror-twins of their hiragana counterparts (か/カ, き/キ, せ/セ) — lean on that.
5. Don't stress perfect handwriting early; recognition comes first.
