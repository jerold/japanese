# Food Etiquette

A short tour of table manners that will keep you from inadvertently reenacting a funeral ritual at dinner.

## Before and after eating

- **いただきます** (itadakimasu) — said (often with hands pressed together) before the first bite. "I humbly receive."
- **ごちそうさまでした** (gochisousama deshita) — said after eating. "That was a feast."

Both are nearly universal in Japan. Even alone at home, many people say them softly.

## Chopstick (箸 hashi) rules

A few chopstick behaviors carry funeral associations and are strong taboos:
1. **Do not stick chopsticks upright in a bowl of rice** — this mirrors incense offered to the dead.
2. **Do not pass food chopstick-to-chopstick** — this also echoes a funerary bone-passing rite.

Other etiquette:
- Don't point at people with chopsticks.
- Don't spear food (except in extremity — e.g., picking up something slippery).
- Don't drag bowls around using chopsticks.
- When sharing, use the opposite ends (or serving utensils if provided).
- Chopstick rest (箸置き hashi-oki): if one is provided, use it. Otherwise rest them across your bowl.

## Bowl handling

- It's **encouraged** to pick up smaller bowls (rice, miso soup) and bring them close to your mouth. This is the opposite of Western etiquette.
- Don't pick up larger shared plates or the main dish.

## Noodles

- **Slurping** noodles — ramen, udon, soba — is acceptable and can even signal enjoyment. A quiet slurp is fine, but slurping isn't required.
- Western etiquette dictates against; Japanese etiquette shrugs.

## Drinking together

- **Never pour your own drink.** Pour for others; they'll pour for you.
- Hold the bottle or pitcher with both hands when pouring as a sign of respect to elders or superiors.
- The toast is **乾杯！** (kanpai!) — "cheers!"
- Wait until everyone has a drink before the first sip.

## Tipping

- **No tipping** — anywhere. Restaurants, taxis, hotels. It can actually cause awkwardness. Service is included in the price.

## Small end-of-meal habits

- Stack plates neatly if eating casually (some find this polite, some unnecessary — follow local cues).
- Say ごちそうさまでした on leaving, especially to the chef/server.
