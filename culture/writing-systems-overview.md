# Writing Systems Overview

Japanese uses three scripts simultaneously. A typical sentence mixes all three, and the choice of script often carries meaning on its own.

## The three scripts

### ひらがな (hiragana)

- 46 basic characters (plus dakuten/handakuten variants and combined yōon).
- **Phonetic** — each character is one syllable/mora.
- Used for: native Japanese words without assigned kanji, grammatical particles (は, を, に…), verb and adjective endings, and as a fallback when a kanji is too obscure.
- Shapes are curvy, cursive-feeling.

### カタカナ (katakana)

- Same 46 sounds as hiragana, in a different (angular) script.
- Used for: foreign loanwords (コーヒー koohii, "coffee"), foreign names (ジェロルド Jerorudo, "Jerold"), scientific names, onomatopoeia, and emphasis (like italics).
- Shapes look more sharp-edged; many derived from simplified kanji strokes.

### 漢字 (kanji)

- Thousands of logographs borrowed from Chinese over centuries.
- Each kanji typically has multiple readings — **on'yomi** (音読み, Chinese-derived) and **kun'yomi** (訓読み, native Japanese).
- Carry meaning, not just sound. The same syllable might be written with different kanji for different meanings: 雨 (ame, rain) vs 飴 (ame, candy).
- Schoolchildren learn ~2,000 (the 常用漢字, jōyō kanji) by the end of high school.

## How they combine in a sentence

> 私は毎日学校で日本語を勉強します。
> (watashi wa mainichi gakkou de nihongo wo benkyou shimasu.)

Broken down:
- 私 (watashi) — kanji, content word
- は — hiragana, particle
- 毎日 (mainichi) — kanji, content word
- 学校 (gakkou) — kanji, content word
- で — hiragana, particle
- 日本語 (nihongo) — kanji, content word
- を — hiragana, particle
- 勉強 (benkyou) — kanji, noun-stem
- します (shimasu) — hiragana, verb ending (the する stem is sometimes written in kana, sometimes in kanji 為)

The pattern: **content words in kanji, grammatical glue in hiragana.** Katakana slots in when there's a foreign word.

## Reading strategies for beginners

1. Master hiragana first (phase 1 days 1–5).
2. Add katakana (phase 1 days 4–10).
3. Absorb kanji slowly — in phase 1, most vocab is presented in kanji + kana + romaji so you see both forms. In phase 2, romaji drops away and you start to read from the kanji directly.
4. Don't panic at kanji you don't know; the hiragana around them often tells you the grammatical function. The meaning becomes patterned over time.

## Furigana

Small hiragana printed above kanji to give the reading — common in children's books, manga, and beginner materials. Leverage these shamelessly.

## Rōmaji

The Roman-alphabet transcription used in signs, passports, and beginner materials. Two common systems:
- **Hepburn** (used in this project) — closer to English-speaker intuition. し = shi, つ = tsu, ふ = fu.
- **Kunrei-shiki** — more internally consistent to Japanese. し = si, つ = tu, ふ = hu.

Stick with Hepburn; drop rōmaji once the kana feel natural.
