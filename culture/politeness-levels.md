# Politeness Levels

Japanese encodes politeness directly into its grammar — the same idea is expressed with different verb forms depending on social distance. Understanding the broad strokes helps you not over- or under-shoot.

## The three rough registers

1. **Casual / plain (タメ口 tameguchi)** — used with close friends, family, children, and pets. Verbs end in dictionary form (食べる, 行く), copula is だ.
2. **Polite (丁寧語 teineigo)** — the default for adult strangers, coworkers, shopkeepers, teachers, classmates you don't know well. Verbs in ～ます, copula です. **This is your phase 1 register.**
3. **Honorific / humble (敬語 keigo)** — a complex two-pronged system used in business, customer service, and toward people much higher in social standing. You'll meet it in phase 2 and beyond.

## When to use which

A safe heuristic: **default to polite (です/ます) until invited to switch.** Many people, especially close in age, will eventually say something like "敬語じゃなくていいよ" ("you don't have to be so polite") — that's your cue.

## How register shifts a sentence

Take "I went to school yesterday."

- Casual: 昨日学校に行った。(kinou gakkou ni itta.)
- Polite: 昨日学校に行きました。(kinou gakkou ni ikimashita.)
- Honorific (about someone else): 昨日学校にいらっしゃいました。(kinou gakkou ni irasshaimashita.)
- Humble (about yourself, formal): 昨日学校に伺いました。(kinou gakkou ni ukagaimashita.)

Same meaning, different social posture. As a beginner you only need rows 1 and 2 — but recognize the others when you hear them.

## Honorific お and ご

You'll see お (o-) or ご (go-) prefixed to certain nouns to add politeness:
- お茶 (ocha, tea), お水 (omizu, water), お名前 (onamae, name), お元気 (ogenki, well).
- ご家族 (gokazoku, family), ご飯 (gohan, rice/meal — yes, this one is fossilized).

Use the honorific form when **referring to the listener's** thing or when the word is conventionally always honorified (お茶, ご飯). Drop it when describing your own.

## A few cultural rules of thumb

- Slightly over-polite is almost always safer than under-polite.
- Politeness in Japanese is about *position*, not friendliness. You can be warm and polite, or curt and casual.
- Don't use honorific verbs about yourself or your own group — that's the role of *humble* forms.
