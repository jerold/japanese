# Bowing & Greetings Etiquette

Bowing (お辞儀 ojigi) is the physical vocabulary of Japanese politeness. You don't need perfect form to get by — just a cooperative posture signals respect.

## Three everyday bows (rough rule of thumb)

| Bow | Angle | When |
|---|---|---|
| 会釈 (eshaku) | ~15° | Casual greeting, thanks in passing, acknowledging someone at the office. |
| 敬礼 (keirei) | ~30° | Standard polite bow. Meeting someone, thanks to a shopkeeper, business introductions. |
| 最敬礼 (saikeirei) | ~45°+ | Deep thanks, serious apology, very formal settings. |

For learners, a **soft nod-to-shallow-bow** covers almost every interaction.

## How to bow

- Keep your back straight — bend from the waist, not the neck.
- Men: hands at sides. Women: hands clasped in front.
- Eyes: generally down, not meeting the other person's during the bow.
- Rhythm: slightly slow down, then rise at a similar pace. A rushed bow reads as insincere.

## Don't shake hands unless prompted

Westerners default to extending a hand; Japanese people default to bowing. Start with a bow, and mirror the other person if they offer a handshake. Business cards (名刺 meishi) come with their own handling ritual, covered in phase 2.

## Greetings are reciprocal — usually

When someone bows or greets you, you bow/greet back. Some exceptions:
- Staff greeting you in a shop (いらっしゃいませ) — you don't need to respond. A small nod is warm.
- A very deep bow of apology from a stranger — a small acknowledging bow is fine; you don't need to match the depth.

## Signature phrase + bow combinations

- よろしくお願いします + 30° bow — meeting someone for the first time.
- ありがとうございました + 30° bow — completing a transaction.
- 失礼します + small bow — leaving a room or meeting.
