# Name Suffixes — さん, くん, ちゃん, 様, 先生

Japanese almost never uses someone's bare name. The suffix you attach signals your relationship and respect level. Get the suffix wrong and the name itself sounds wrong.

## さん (-san)

Default polite suffix. Use with adults you don't know well, coworkers, neighbors, classmates. Works for any gender.
- 田中さん (Tanaka-san) — Mr/Ms Tanaka.
- 山田さん (Yamada-san) — Mr/Ms Yamada.

When in doubt, use ～さん.

## くん (-kun)

Less formal than さん. Often used for:
- Young men or boys, especially by older people or seniors.
- Male peers in a casual workplace.
- Sometimes used for women in workplace contexts by superiors.
- Occasionally between close friends.

A teacher might call all students by ～くん regardless of gender in some traditions.

## ちゃん (-chan)

Affectionate / cute. Used for:
- Children (especially girls).
- Close friends, often female.
- Pets.
- Nicknames among friends.

Using ～ちゃん with a stranger is overly familiar. Even within friend groups, get a sense of when it's welcome before using it.

## 様 (-sama)

Very formal / deferential. Used for:
- Customers (the famous お客様 okyaku-sama).
- People of high status in formal writing.
- The names of gods/spirits (神様 kami-sama).

Don't use ～様 in casual conversation; you'll sound like you're addressing a feudal lord.

## 先生 (-sensei)

Used as a *title*, not a generic suffix. Replaces ～さん for:
- Teachers.
- Doctors.
- Lawyers.
- Authors / artists / musicians of standing.
- Martial-arts instructors.

You'd never say "Tanaka-sensei-san"; the title alone is enough respect.

## 呼び捨て (yobisute) — calling someone by name only

Dropping the suffix is intimate and assumes a close relationship. Doing this with someone you don't know well sounds rude. Family members and very close friends can use bare first names; strangers cannot.

## On yourself

Never attach an honorific to your own name. You're 田中 (Tanaka), not 田中さん, when introducing yourself.
