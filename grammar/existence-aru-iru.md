# ある vs いる — Verbs of Existence

Japanese has two verbs for "to be (existing somewhere)," split by whether the thing in question is alive.

- **ある** — for inanimate objects and abstract things (books, houses, weather, plans, meetings).
- **いる** — for animate things (people, animals, even a sleeping person is still animate).

Plants are traditionally treated as inanimate → ある.

## The pattern

```
[place] に [thing] が ある / いる
```

- 部屋に机**が**あります。(heya ni tsukue ga arimasu.) = "There is a desk in the room."
- 公園に子供**が**います。(kouen ni kodomo ga imasu.) = "There are children in the park."

Notice the particles: the **place** takes に, the **thing being said to exist** takes が.

## Reverse pattern (topicalizing the thing)

You can flip the word order and topicalize the thing with は:
- 机は部屋にあります。= "The desk is in the room."

Both orderings are fine; pick based on what's old vs new information.

## Polite forms

| Plain | Polite | Negative |
|---|---|---|
| ある | あります | ありません |
| いる | います | いません |

## "I have X"

Japanese uses ある/いる for possession too — there's no separate verb like English "have":
- 車**が**あります。(kuruma ga arimasu.) = "I have a car." (lit. "A car exists.")
- 兄弟**が**います。(kyoudai ga imasu.) = "I have siblings."

## Memory trick

- 生きて**いる** things → **いる** (the verb shares the い).
- Not alive → **ある**.
