# i-Adjectives

i-adjectives are Japanese's "real" adjectives — they end in い in dictionary form and carry their own conjugation. Unlike in English, an i-adjective changes shape to show tense and negation; です does *not* do this work.

## Forms

Using 高い (takai, "expensive / tall") as the template:

| Form | Ending | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Non-past | ～い | 高い (takai) |
| Negative | ～くない | 高くない (takakunai) |
| Past | ～かった | 高かった (takakatta) |
| Negative past | ～くなかった | 高くなかった (takakunakatta) |

For politeness, add です at the very end — and *never* conjugate です itself on an i-adjective.

- 高いです。(takai desu.) = "It's expensive."
- 高くないです。(takakunai desu.) = "It's not expensive."
- 高かったです。(takakatta desu.) = "It was expensive."
- 高くなかったです。(takakunakatta desu.) = "It wasn't expensive."

## Common i-adjectives

いい (ii), 悪い (warui), 大きい (ookii), 小さい (chiisai), 新しい (atarashii), 古い (furui), 高い (takai), 安い (yasui), 美味しい (oishii), 楽しい (tanoshii), 暑い (atsui, hot), 寒い (samui, cold).

## Before nouns

i-adjectives attach directly to a noun — no particle needed:
- 大きい家 (ookii ie) = "a big house"
- 新しい車 (atarashii kuruma) = "a new car"

## The irregular one: いい

**いい** (ii, "good") borrows its negative and past forms from its older root **よい** (yoi):
- Negative: よくない (yokunai), not *ii-kunai*.
- Past: よかった (yokatta), not *iikatta*.
- よかった！ alone = "phew!" / "thank goodness!" — culturally common reaction.

## Compound warning

A trap for English speakers: the ～い ending makes some nouns *look* like adjectives but they aren't.
- 好き (suki, "like / liked") — actually a **na**-adjective, despite ending in い.
- 嫌い (kirai, "dislike") — same story.

If in doubt, look the word up rather than guess.
