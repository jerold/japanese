# を — Direct Object Marker

**を** marks the direct object — the thing being acted on by the verb. Pronounced simply `o` in modern Japanese (the `w` is silent).

## Pattern

```
[Subject は] [Object を] [verb]。
```

- パンを食べます。(pan wo tabemasu.) = "I eat bread."
- 本を読みます。(hon wo yomimasu.) = "I read a book."
- 日本語を話します。(nihongo wo hanashimasu.) = "I speak Japanese."

## When to use を

Almost any time English would have a transitive verb with a direct object:
- "I drink coffee" → コーヒー**を**飲みます。
- "I see the movie" → 映画**を**見ます。
- "I write a letter" → 手紙**を**書きます。

## Tricky verbs that *don't* take を

A few verbs feel transitive in English but use が in Japanese:
- **わかる** (to understand) → 日本語**が**わかります。 (not *を*)
- **好き** (like — actually a na-adjective) → 寿司**が**好きです。
- **できる** (can do) → 料理**が**できます。

This is because these are framed as states ("Japanese is understandable to me"), not actions.

## Movement verbs and を

When you go *through* or *along* a place, that place takes を rather than で:
- 公園を歩きます。(kouen wo arukimasu.) = "I walk through the park."
- 橋を渡ります。(hashi wo watarimasu.) = "I cross the bridge."

This sometimes surprises learners — yes, the path of motion is the "object" of the motion verb in Japanese.
