# na-Adjectives

na-adjectives behave like nouns — they do *not* conjugate themselves. Instead, tense and negation live on です (or in casual speech, だ / じゃない).

## Common na-adjectives

元気 (genki, energetic/well), 静か (shizuka, quiet), きれい (kirei, pretty/clean), 好き (suki, liked), 嫌い (kirai, disliked), 有名 (yuumei, famous), 便利 (benri, convenient), 親切 (shinsetsu, kind).

Note that きれい and 嫌い end in い visually but are na-adjectives. The underlying hiragana is the test, not the appearance.

## Before a noun — add な

When a na-adjective modifies a noun, glue them together with な.
- 静か**な**部屋 (shizuka na heya) = "a quiet room"
- きれい**な**花 (kirei na hana) = "pretty flower(s)"
- 有名**な**人 (yuumei na hito) = "a famous person"

That な is literally where the name "na-adjective" comes from.

## Predicating — use です

A na-adjective at the end of a sentence just takes です — no な.
- この部屋は静かです。(kono heya wa shizuka desu.) = "This room is quiet."
- 田中さんは親切です。(Tanaka-san wa shinsetsu desu.) = "Tanaka-san is kind."

## Negative and past — on です, not on the adjective

Because na-adjectives are noun-like, conjugation lives on です:

| Form | Example |
|---|---|
| Non-past | 静かです (shizuka desu) |
| Negative | 静かじゃないです (shizuka ja nai desu) |
| Past | 静かでした (shizuka deshita) |
| Negative past | 静かじゃなかったです (shizuka ja nakatta desu) |

Contrast this with i-adjectives, which conjugate *themselves*. See [i-adjectives](./i-adjectives.md).

## The 好き / 嫌い pattern

好き (suki, "liked") and 嫌い (kirai, "disliked") are na-adjectives. The thing liked takes が, not を — because grammatically the sentence says "X is likeable (to me)":

- 寿司**が**好きです。(sushi ga suki desu.) = "I like sushi." (lit. "Sushi is liked.")
- コーヒー**が**嫌いです。(koohii ga kirai desu.) = "I dislike coffee."
