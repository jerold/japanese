# は vs が — Topic vs Subject

The classic Japanese confusion. The short version: **は sets the stage; が identifies the actor or new information.** You can go a long way with the heuristics below and refine your intuition over time.

## は (wa) — Topic marker

Think of は as "*as for* X" — it names what you're about to talk about.
- 私は学生です。(watashi wa gakusei desu.) = "As for me, I'm a student."
- 今日は暑いです。(kyou wa atsui desu.) = "As for today, it's hot."

The topic is often already known from context. You drop it once it's obvious.

**Spelling note:** the particle `は` is pronounced **wa**, not *ha*. This is a historical holdover. Same goes for へ (pronounced `e`) and を (pronounced `o`).

## が (ga) — Subject marker

が marks the grammatical subject, and highlights *new* or *important* information — often the answer to an implicit question.
- 猫がいます。(neko ga imasu.) = "There's a cat." (introducing the cat — new info.)
- 誰が来ましたか？(dare ga kimashita ka?) = "Who came?" (answer: → 田中さん**が**来ました — "Tanaka-san came." The new info is *who*.)

## Quick heuristics

1. **First mention / introducing something new → が.** Subsequent references in the same conversation → は.
   > 昔々、おじいさん**が**いました。おじいさん**は**山に行きました。
   > "Long ago, there was an old man. The old man went to the mountain."

2. **Question words (誰, 何, どこ…) take が; their answers also take が.**
   > 何**が**好きですか？ — 寿司**が**好きです。

3. **Contrast → は.** "X は ~ですが、Y は…"
   > コーヒーは好きですが、紅茶は好きじゃないです。
   > "I like coffee, but I don't like black tea."

4. **ある / いる (existence) usually use が** for the thing existing.
   > 部屋に机**が**あります。(There's a desk in the room.)

5. **"Is / equals" statements (identification) usually use は.**
   > あの人**は**先生です。(That person is a teacher.)

## A famous test case

> 象は鼻が長い。(zou wa hana ga nagai.)
> "As for elephants, (their) nose is long."

- 象は — *topic* = "elephants"
- 鼻が — *subject* = "nose"
- 長い — "is long"

You can't translate this with one subject in English. Japanese lets you frame a topic and describe some sub-feature with its own subject marker — and this structure is very common.
