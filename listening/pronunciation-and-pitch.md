# Pronunciation & Pitch

The good news: Japanese pronunciation is far more regular than English. Each kana maps to essentially one sound. The harder parts — long vowels, pitch accent, and syllable rhythm — are subtle but learnable.

## Vowels

Five pure vowels, short and clean:
- **あ (a)** — like "a" in "father."
- **い (i)** — like "ee" in "see."
- **う (u)** — roughly "oo" in "soon" but unrounded (don't purse your lips).
- **え (e)** — like "e" in "pet" but a touch more open.
- **お (o)** — like "o" in "so" without the w-glide English often adds.

No diphthongs. Two vowels next to each other are pronounced as two separate beats, not slid together.

## Long vowels matter

Holding a vowel twice as long can change the word:
- おばさん (obasan) = "aunt"
- おばあさん (obaasan) = "grandmother"

Practice clocks: count one beat for the short, two beats for the long.

## Consonants worth noting

- **r** row (ら/り/る/れ/ろ) — a tap, somewhere between English r, l, and d. Tap the roof of your mouth lightly.
- **f** in ふ — softer than English f; made with both lips (like blowing out a candle).
- **tsu** in つ — one syllable, not "t + soo." Practice saying "cats!" without the "ca."

## Devoiced vowels

In standard Tokyo Japanese, **i** and **u** between voiceless consonants (k, s, t, p, h) are often whispered or nearly silent:
- 好き (suki) often sounds like "s'ki."
- です often sounds like "des."

Don't worry about producing this at first; just know why it sounds like native speakers are "swallowing" vowels.

## Pitch accent

Japanese uses **pitch accent** rather than stress accent. Each word has a pitch pattern that can change meaning:

- 箸 (hashi) = "chopsticks" — high-low pattern (HA-shi).
- 橋 (hashi) = "bridge" — low-high pattern (ha-SHI).
- 端 (hashi) = "edge" — low-high, but the particle following it is low (ha-SHI, particle drops).

Dictionaries mark pitch with numbers (0, 1, 2…) where 0 means "flat" and n > 0 means "drops after the n-th mora."

As a learner, **don't obsess over pitch** for your first year. Listening practice bakes it in naturally. Glaring mistakes will be understood from context.

## The mora is the unit

Japanese rhythm is **mora-timed**, not syllable-timed like English. Each kana is one mora and takes one beat:
- さ-く-ら (sa-ku-ra) = 3 beats.
- とう-きょう (tou-kyou) = 4 beats. Each long vowel is two beats.
- がっ-こう (gak-kou) = 4 beats. Each っ/ッ is a beat of silence.

Clap along to words to feel it.
