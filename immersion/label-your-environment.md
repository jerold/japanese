# Label Your Environment

A small, physical trick that punches above its weight. Stick Japanese labels on objects in your home and office so vocabulary becomes part of the wallpaper of your life.

## What to label

Start with 15–25 common objects in rooms you use daily:

### Living room / general
- 机 (つくえ, tsukue) — desk
- 椅子 (いす, isu) — chair
- 本 (ほん, hon) — book
- ドア (doa) — door
- 窓 (まど, mado) — window
- 電気 (でんき, denki) — light / electricity

### Kitchen
- 水 (みず, mizu) — water
- お茶 (おちゃ, ocha) — tea
- ご飯 (ごはん, gohan) — rice / meal
- 冷蔵庫 (れいぞうこ, reizouko) — refrigerator
- 皿 (さら, sara) — plate
- コップ (koppu) — cup

### Bedroom
- ベッド (beddo) — bed
- 枕 (まくら, makura) — pillow
- 時計 (とけい, tokei) — clock

### Bathroom
- 鏡 (かがみ, kagami) — mirror
- 歯ブラシ (はブラシ, ha-burashi) — toothbrush
- タオル (taoru) — towel

## How to format each label

Three lines, big enough to read from a few feet away:

```
冷蔵庫
れいぞうこ
reizouko — fridge
```

In phase 1, include romaji. Once the kana feels natural (late phase 1 or phase 2), drop romaji. Eventually drop hiragana for items whose kanji you know.

## What this actually does

You are not memorizing the labels by reading them once. You are exposing yourself to each word **dozens of times per day** in the context where it's used. That contextual repetition is how the word sticks without effort.

## Rotate

After ~2 weeks on any label, start quizzing yourself: cover the reading, try to recall it. Once you know a word cold, replace the label with a new one. Keep ~20 labels live at any time.

## Extend to a phrase zone

Pick one spot (fridge door, bathroom mirror) and tape a **phrase** you're trying to internalize:
- 「今日もよろしくお願いします」 on the door you leave through.
- 「お疲れ様」 near your desk.
- 「美味しい！」 in the kitchen.

Say it aloud when you pass. Micro-practice, zero scheduling overhead.
